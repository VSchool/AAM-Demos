Somewhere along the way, cast iron became precious. People treat their skillets like heirlooms that might shatter if you look at them wrong. They won't use soap. They won't cook tomatoes. They store them wrapped in cloth like medieval artifacts.

Here's the thing: cast iron is a chunk of metal. It survived being poured from molten iron at 2,100°F. Your pasta sauce is not going to hurt it.

## What Seasoning Actually Is

Seasoning isn't a coating you apply — it's a chemical change in the metal's surface. When fat heats past its smoke point on cast iron, the fatty acids undergo polymerization. They bond to the iron and to each other, forming a hard, smooth, plastic-like surface.

This surface is:
- **Hydrophobic** (repels water, which prevents rust)
- **Slick** (reduces friction, which prevents sticking)  
- **Self-healing** (every time you cook with fat, you add to it)

The key word is *polymerization*. It's the same chemical process that makes paint harden. Once polymerized, those fats aren't going anywhere — certainly not from a drop of dish soap.

## The Soap Myth

Old-school soap contained lye, which is a strong enough base to strip polymerized oil. Modern dish soap (Dawn, for example) is a gentle surfactant. It removes loose grease and food particles. It does not touch polymerized seasoning.

Wash your cast iron with soap if it needs it. Dry it thoroughly. That's it.

## The Tomato Myth

Acidic foods can interact with bare cast iron, producing a metallic taste and degrading the seasoning. On a well-seasoned pan, this isn't an issue. The polymerized layer protects the iron.

If your pan is new or recently stripped, yes — avoid acidic foods until you've built up several layers of seasoning. But a pan that's been in regular use for a few months? Make all the tomato sauce you want.

## How to Actually Season Cast Iron

**The oven method** (for building base layers):

1. Wash the pan with soap and water. Dry completely.
2. Apply a thin — *thin* — layer of flaxseed oil, vegetable oil, or Crisco. Wipe it until it looks like you removed all the oil. There should be no visible sheen.
3. Place upside down in a 500°F oven for one hour.
4. Let it cool in the oven.
5. Repeat 3-4 times.

**The daily method** (for maintaining and improving):

Cook with it. Use fat. Don't be shy about heat. Every time you sear a steak, fry an egg, or roast vegetables, you're adding to the seasoning.

## If You Ruin It

You probably can't, but let's say you do. Let's say you left it soaking overnight and it's covered in rust. Here's the fix:

1. Scrub the rust off with steel wool or a chainmail scrubber.
2. Wash with soap and water.
3. Dry immediately and thoroughly.
4. Re-season using the oven method above.

Total recovery time: one afternoon. Your grandmother's skillet has survived worse.

## The Best Thing About Cast Iron

It gets better with use. Every other piece of cookware in your kitchen is slowly degrading — non-stick coatings wear away, stainless steel warps, aluminum oxidizes. Cast iron goes the other direction. The more you cook with it, the better it performs.

That skillet you bought for $25 will outperform a $200 non-stick pan within a year of regular use. And it'll still be working when the non-stick pan is in a landfill.
