I spent years thinking knife skills meant speed. Watch any cooking show and the chef is blurring through an onion, the knife a silver streak. That's impressive. It's also irrelevant to making good food.

What actually matters is consistency. Pieces that are the same size cook at the same rate. That's it. That's the whole reason knife skills exist.

## The Brunoise (Fine Dice)

A brunoise is a 3mm cube. You'll use it for mirepoix, salsa, relish — anything where you want ingredients to disappear into the dish rather than stand out.

**The method**: Start with a flat surface. If you're dicing an onion, cut it in half through the root. Lay it flat-side down. Make horizontal cuts toward the root (but don't cut through it — the root holds everything together). Then make vertical cuts. Finally, cut across to release the dice.

**Why it matters**: A proper brunoise means your soffritto cooks evenly. No burnt bits, no raw chunks. The vegetables melt into the sauce, creating a foundation rather than a texture.

## The Chiffonade (Ribbon Cut)

Stack your leaves (basil, mint, spinach, whatever), roll them into a tight cylinder, and slice thin cross-sections. The leaves unfurl into delicate ribbons.

**The method**: The key is the roll. Too loose and the leaves shift, giving you uneven cuts. Too tight and you bruise the herbs, which turns them black. Medium pressure, sharp knife, long slicing strokes rather than chopping.

**Why it matters**: Chiffonade distributes herbs evenly across a dish. Torn basil clumps. Chopped basil bruises. Chiffonade basil drapes over pasta or salad like confetti, releasing aroma gradually rather than all at once.

## The Bias Cut (Diagonal)

Hold your knife at a 45-degree angle to the vegetable and cut. That's it. The simplest cut on this list and arguably the most useful.

**The method**: Works on any cylindrical vegetable — carrots, celery, scallions, asparagus. The angle creates an oval cross-section instead of a circle, which means more surface area.

**Why it matters**: More surface area means more browning. More browning means more flavor (thanks, Maillard reaction). A bias-cut carrot in a stir-fry develops twice the caramelization of a straight-cut carrot. It also looks better on the plate, which shouldn't matter but does.

## The Real Skill

None of these cuts require speed. A brunoise at one onion per minute is no better than a brunoise at five minutes per onion, as long as the pieces are consistent. Speed comes naturally with repetition. Consistency comes from paying attention.

Get a sharp knife. A truly sharp knife — one that slides through a tomato under its own weight. Dull knives are dangerous because they require force, and force means less control. Sharpen your knife every few uses with a honing steel, and take it to a professional sharpener twice a year.

Then practice. One onion a day, brunoise. One bunch of basil, chiffonade. One carrot, bias cut. In two weeks, you'll be measurably faster. In a month, you won't think about it anymore. That's when cooking starts to feel effortless.
