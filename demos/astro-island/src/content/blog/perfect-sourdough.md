There's a moment around hour forty-eight when the dough stops looking like dough and starts looking like something alive. The surface bubbles. The texture shifts from dense paste to something pillowy and slightly resistant. If you've been patient enough to get here, the hardest part is almost over.

## Why 72 Hours?

Most sourdough recipes call for 12 to 24 hours of fermentation. That works. You'll get bread. But extending the timeline to 72 hours does something remarkable to both flavor and texture.

During those extra hours, the wild yeast and lactobacillus bacteria in your starter break down complex starches into simpler sugars. The lactic acid builds slowly, creating a tang that's deep and complex rather than sharp and one-dimensional. The gluten network develops more fully, which means better structure with less kneading.

## The Timeline

**Hours 0-12: The Mix**

Combine flour, water, salt, and active starter. The ratio matters less than you think — most successful sourdough falls between 65% and 80% hydration. I use 75% because it's forgiving. Mix until there are no dry spots, then walk away.

**Hours 12-24: The First Rise**

This is where most recipes tell you to shape and bake. Resist. The dough should have roughly doubled, and you'll see bubbles forming beneath the surface. Perform one set of stretch-and-folds — four pulls from each side, tucking the dough over itself. Then back to the counter, covered.

**Hours 24-48: The Cold Stage**

Move the dough to the refrigerator. This slows fermentation without stopping it. The cold environment favors lactic acid production over acetic acid, which means you get tang without sourness. The gluten continues to develop passively.

**Hours 48-72: The Shape and Final Proof**

Remove from the fridge, shape gently (the dough should practically shape itself at this point), and proof for the remaining time at room temperature. You'll know it's ready when a poke with your finger leaves an indent that springs back slowly — not immediately, not staying put.

## The Bake

Preheat your Dutch oven to 500°F. Score the top of the loaf with a razor blade or sharp knife — this controls where the bread expands. Drop the temperature to 450°F when the bread goes in. Lid on for 20 minutes, lid off for another 25.

The crust should be deeply caramelized, almost mahogany. Tap the bottom — it should sound hollow. Then comes the hardest part of the entire process: waiting an hour for it to cool before cutting.

## What You'll Notice

The crumb will be open and irregular — large holes interspersed with smaller ones. The flavor will have layers: wheaty sweetness, mild tang, a hint of nuttiness from the long fermentation. The crust will crackle when you squeeze it.

Three days is a long time to wait for bread. But once you taste the difference, twelve-hour sourdough starts to feel like a shortcut.
